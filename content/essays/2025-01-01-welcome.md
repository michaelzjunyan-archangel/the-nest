The Unicorn Tapestries: My Read on Willing submission into Captivity

*The Unicorn Tapestries or the Hunt of the Unicorn (French: La Chasse à la licorne) is a series of seven tapestries made in the Southern Netherlands around 1495–1505, and now in The Cloisters in New York City. They were possibly designed in Paris and woven in Brussels. They depict a group of noblemen and hunters in pursuit of a unicorn through an idealised French landscape. The tapestries were woven in wool, metallic threads, and silk. The vibrant colours, still evident today, were produced from dye plants: weld (yellow), madder (red), and woad (blue). Wikipedia.*

My first encounter with this work traces back three years, to late 2022 when preparing for an academic pentathlon that took me down a rabbit trail of fine arts. Of all the genres and hundreds of pieces covered, I saved this one and only this one on my laptop. I was transfixed by this pinnacle of medieval tapestry in its workmanship more than any theme it may have represented.

Scholars believe that it points at an allegory of Christ and the Virgin Mary. The parallel is certainly there, in the willing entrapment of the unicorn there is a mirroring of Christ's death on the cross. Either way, scholars mostly agree that the unicorn represents the rare and the extraordinary, just as Christ is fully human and yet above humanity. The crucifixion of Christ on the cross for humanity is a similar, voluntary sacrifice to the unicorn surrendering itself to the virgin's lap. 

But I’m not here to dispute or add fuel to that conversation. I’m neither at a position of knowledge on this particular topic nor do I believe there’s much more to be definitively read, since the exact origins of the piece are long lost. However, I do have two unique reads from distinct perspectives that the piece evoked from me.

1) In a sense, we are the unicorn. With the rise of the unicorn as a symbol in queer culture, it’s hard not to make the connection. While acceptance is increasingly prevalent in the United States and Europe, there is an underlying feeling of being a “caged bird”, so to say. As the unicorn is admired and hunted for its rarity and beauty, so are queer minorities similarly contained, examined with more scrutiny, and made an object of spectacle. To extrapolate this point, we risk becoming the “unicorn” anywhere we are special, and where we differ in a truly meaningful way. When uniqueness becomes justification for capture and containment, the only societal norm becomes to not have any personality beyond what normalcy dictates. 

Yet, the unicorn gives itself into the maiden’s lap. It is incredibly striking imagery (though unfortunately the tapestry depicting this is fragmented and the subject is lost) It is easy to argue whether this is deception, voluntary submission, or something else entirely. I find the second interpretation compelling. We, as the unicorn, give in for comfort. We stop flying our colors so openly. We stop correcting others every time they make a misassumption. We give in or deflect to probing questions not because it is what suits us best, but rather gives a sense of complacency within the system. By not disputing anything and minimizing friction, it is easier to believe that we have been accepted. Truthfully, we remove ourselves from the pedestal of uniqueness and shroud in cover of the bland side that society has asked of us. We relegate ourselves to cage our own identity in exchange for an ease in living. The bind is not absolute, just as the unicorn is not chained tightly. 

2)We are the Hunters. The concept of unicorns are oddly redolent of whale hunting for me. Both are mysterious, potent creatures certainly more powerful than one meager human. Yet, as a species, we choose not to venerate or respect them but rather hunt and drive them into danger. When I approach this problem, it is easy for me to fall into an assumption that these actions are driven by a twisted spite and some sort of anger that brings us to raise our spears at majestic creatures beyond our control. However, in real life, we just as much may have done these acts out of self-interest. It is somehow more striking, more cold, to think that the lives of these living things were lost to bring about better numbers on a page or brighter oil for the lamps. It is hard to renounce systemic violence in our wreckage of other animals, ecosystems, and every living thing when it is seemingly just survival, but taken to an extreme. And bad guys don’t say they’re bad guys. Even when they’re self-conscious.

Anthropology does not offer up a good enough explanation for this greed that appears uniquely human. It is insufficient to claim humans are evil, when truthfully all living things are wired by nature to serve their own purposes. Perhaps it is only seen to be so prevalent because only humans have reached such a level of control–and development of such complex systems–that greed and excessive spite is able to present itself. In a word, we are motivated by systems and incentives rather than hatred towards anything. It is a lot harder to not be self-centric when there’s more than two bananas and a lick of water at stake. 

In either case, one thing is certain: The unicorn is not free. Even in usage of the term in pop culture and terms like “unicorn hunting” in queer culture, the “unicorn” is meant to be of subservience to the unmentioned denominator, the mass of humans that are, for the lack of a better word, painstakingly ordinary. The undertone is that of recognizing individual inferiority, but yet choosing to overpower the anomaly into submission as an amalgamated whole for one’s own interests and pride. 

Perhaps the enduring power of the tapestries lies in this uncomfortable duality. At different moments, we are both the unicorn and the hunter—creatures capable of surrendering our own freedom for comfort while simultaneously participating in the systems that capture others.
