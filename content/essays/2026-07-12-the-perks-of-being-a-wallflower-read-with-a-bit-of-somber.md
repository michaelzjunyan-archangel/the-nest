I’ve held off on talking about this one. But there are certain qualities that this book masters quietly, that make it less of a novel and more of a glimpse into a parallel world where the story actually happened. And no, I had not watched the motion picture. Yet.  



The setting is in the 1990s US suburbs. The main character, Charlie, is fresh into high school. At face value, the entire backdrop is most redolent for me of something like Diary of a Wimpy Kid, or maybe The Outsiders. Suburbia with a tinge of youthful cliches and early Y2K nostalgia. As part of a generation that has largely lived entirely in tandem with things like mobile phones, social media, and, now, generative artificial intelligence, It’s hard not to find the “old days” charming in an idyllic way. But Stephen Chbosky, having written this book in 1999 at the eve of that turn of millenia, did not hesitate to paint that era in both its bright and bleak colors impartially. As a generation, we tend to think that concepts like trauma, mental health, and just being a confused high schooler in general are rather modern implements, but that couldn’t be farther from the truth.



The narrative is framed through a series of letters by Charlie sent anonymously to a friend that isn’t named. More than a tool to ground the narrative, the letters also shape Charlie’s unique and tender first-person tone. Though not to say he is an unreliable narrator, we know only as much as he is willing to articulate in that moment, and more often than not we are led down unexpected tangents that end up uncovering a central part of the plot. It’s like an onion, except a worm bore a winding hole through it instead of peeling it (for whatever reason a worm might want to feed on an onion, of all vegetables) and we end up deeper than ever expected. Even Charlie himself is unaware of some things, and we see only as far as he does. This leaves space for the plot to unfold in ways that make its central themes ever more poignant. A virtuoso use of close third person.



And, to be honest, it feels like someone else always has better things to say when it comes to widely acclaimed works like this one. Especially when reading it after the fact, in the “Big 2026”, when the text has grown to take on different meanings and shift in the public eye as our collective perception has also irrepressibly altered–there is an inherent repulsion for me to try and wrap my tentacles around the full context of this work. (But, again, I didn’t find any trouble reviewing The Aeneid, so)



It is one thing to read it as an adult, but as a teenager, the characterization of Charlie and his experience speaks in distinct ways to parts of growth and coming of age that are too often left behind or covered in broad strokes. The palpable effects of trauma, and anxiety, and depression–is incredibly well illustrated in a form most accessible to teenagers. 



The story is also more timely framed in a tragedy that coincided in my very life. A peer, one of many in my school, took his life right around the time I was starting the book. We were not close, but I saw him enough that I knew what he was like. He was a friend of friends. We didn’t find out until weeks later, and each day we prayed for him on different occasions. Those prayers were some of the most devout I’ve ever had, for what good that’s worth. When his body was found, tests and assignments were dismissed and everyone stayed in the library if they felt like it. It was strange that school was not cancelled, but perhaps that would invoke too much joy—the ironic disregard type—in us insensitive, cynical teenagers. I sat in the dining room and read the book, but I had to stop every few pages. I kept thinking about the chains that bound him, the things he went through mentally that made him finally choose such a radical act. I thought about whether the chains would ever get all of us for good. I thought about why I couldn’t have done anything. I guess I just couldn’t have known. I hope his soul rests in peace.





So, since there is so much to talk about and I’d rather not talk about some of them, I’d rather just zoom in on three particular points in the book.





The Illusion of Nostalgia.



Behind nostalgia is an emotion that is most heavily skewed–one that compels us to document its glories in photographs, music discs, and vague remarks of things purportedly commonplace then which are now scarce and sporadic. But we do not document the silences. We skim over the long nights, and stuff away the truth that there has never been more or less good in our world, but rather the good and bad shift around, switch places, and get blurred as the water under the bridge builds to a river. 



Hereditary Interpretations 



Many general views on the work state the breadth of topics it covers–from rape all the way to domestic violence, mental health, and substance abuse–as either a weakness or most significant strength of the theme delivery. In my own perspective, I can see where both sides are coming from. Sure, the book doesn’t spend time delving in depth or resolving any topic raised up per se, but I believe that is faithful to Charlie’s perspective and the overarching theme of growth and self-identity in the chaos of youth. To use that medium to resolve—or rather, allegorically beat back—these problems would then seem patronizing, stifling the actual theme delivery.







We are Infinite.



This is perhaps the most striking piece of imagery—Charlie standing in the back of the pickup truck, racing through the tunnel, and how they were infinite then. It is intriguing, not only because it utilizes a somewhat circuitous way to describe that limitless, transcendent feeling with a word seldom used that way, but also because of what the statement implicates.



In the end, Charlie is obviously not infinite in the literal sense. He is subject to mortality, and  the irreversible consequences of the maze of things–trauma, memories, and more that he had been subject to—of which many he would’ve liked to not have. But in that moment, connected with his friends that genuinely see and validate him, he is not subject to any of those limits. It is not that these chains which inevitably bound him are broken, but simply loosened. It begs the question: Did that moment of infinity really serve just as a spectacle of escape and ecstasy in a moment of euphoric contentment, or was it truly transcendent in the sense that it incurred a paradigm shift—a change in the very mode of perception from Charlie’s previous upbringing? Both sides are valid.



Conclusion 



So, does the novel argue that a few, fleeting moments are enough to mitigate the effects of perennial suffering?  Are we bound to try and instill deeper, more everlasting meaning in these positive experiences where there is none? Does deep-cutting trauma heal completely in the long run, or does its effects linger even when we feel we are unbounded? The question is left open for us to find out.
