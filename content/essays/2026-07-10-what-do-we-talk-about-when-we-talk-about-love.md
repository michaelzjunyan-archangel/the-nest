The Review of** *What we talk about when we talk about Love***

*photo credit: https://www.amazon.com/What-Talk-About-When-Love/dp/0679723056*

Raymond Carver is one of those writers who leaves a lot of readers feeling slightly stranded when they finish him. What We Talk About When We Talk About Love doesn't really reward the usual reading habits we bring to novels and stories. There is rarely a revelation, a satisfying emotional climax, or a clear statement of what the author wants you to think.

Raymond Carver (1938–1988) was an American short-story writer and poet known for his minimalist style and realistic portrayals of working-class life. Born in Clatskanie, Oregon, and raised in Yakima, Washington, Carver married young and worked a variety of jobs while pursuing his writing career. His stories often focus on ordinary people facing struggles involving relationships, financial hardship, alcoholism, and loneliness. Collections such as Will You Please Be Quiet, Please? (1976), What We Talk About When We Talk About Love (1981), and Cathedral (1983) helped establish him as one of the most influential American short-story writers of the twentieth century. Although his early work is characterized by sparse prose and ambiguity, his later writing became more expansive and hopeful. Carver died of lung cancer in 1988 at the age of fifty. 

Alcohol was the defining struggle of his life, and only after he found sobriety in 1977 was he able to write the most influential works of his career. Still, he takes no effort to avoid this topic—that disordered, messy heap is at the crux of the collection, What We Talk About When We Talk About Love.

When I started my 9th grade year, the Humanities teacher asked us to define justice. The coming fall, he asked us as a class to defend the definition of a chair. We followed Plato, and fought to keep our eyelids open as he used methods to try and get closer to a definition. We never did, because I was waiting for a succinct answer and am still waiting for one.

In Carver’s work, love is justice, or the definitive chair. The characters spend hours talking about it, acting it out, doing stupid things because of it—but they never find out what it means. The collection doesn’t take a stance or force a perspective; It stands like a disinterested stranger at an expo his wife dragged him to. We orbit love in every story, and we never truly leave its gravitational pull. Yet, we never get to the heart of the issue. No effort is made to steer us there. We gently graze its consequences, but we are ultimately taken around it rather than into it.

Carver looks inward to problems, plots, and developments in the ordinary. He writes of late-night wanderings, awkward dinners, and marriages in slow atrophy. He paints a picture of struggles that aren’t desperately dire, of lifestyles questionable but not lethal—and we turn to love to raise the situation, to bleed blue into the black. But the miscommunication, the whims and impulses, and frictions of life–real life–force our romantic optimisms away as the narrative lingers irresolute. No tender affection, unwavering support, or calming trust appeases the unforgiving life that our characters lead When they seek it, they always come back emptier. 

The image Carver paints of the Pacific Northwest here is one characterized by beauty, but more so a gloomy undertow found in geographic isolation, economic insecurity, emotional restraint, and degraded interpersonal relationships. This gives the setting weight in affecting how, and potentially why the characters act in certain ways, and partake in activities like alcoholism or only know peers from work. This is also one of the only things consistent throughout the entire collection.

Despite leaving readers feeling like they just read a collection of random bums’ events Tuesday, Carver actually (really!) weaves much nuance into the short pages and paltry plots of each story. It is arguably one of the most prominent examples of the genre of Literary Minimalism. The meaning is gleaned between the lines, or even beyond the lines. To this style Carver budgets his prose. There are almost no inner activity or backstories, melodramatic soliloquies, or what would usually build a narrative’s depth. Carver’s prose is concise and linear, but not flat. There is more emphasis on what the characters do, and how they do it—often excessively so—while deliberately leaving the “why” blank.

This was especially the case in reading Gazebo, where we spend time knowing the motel they manage, the specific rooms where love gives way to lust, and the wrinkles on the worn-out bedsheets. Taking the events as they are given to us, and thereby forgoing our right to be given an explanation on why they feel this way and where they go from here. 

In Gazebo, we don’t even get the story as it unfolds. It is told in bits and pieces of flashbacks, framed between a few minutes with guests barred from entry bashing on the door. 

In The Bath, ordinary events in a working-class family turns uncanny and filled with dread with a few strokes of plain, unlyrical prose. Not a single “scary” thing fills up the page, but anxiety is left where the words aren’t. 

He has mastered the art of the minimalist short story. Not to adulate Carver, but he puts many full novels to shame in stories where there are no stories at all. Tragedies. Events. Just humans making human noises. Alcohol—always too much of it. I guess that’s what we’re really talking about when we talk about love.
