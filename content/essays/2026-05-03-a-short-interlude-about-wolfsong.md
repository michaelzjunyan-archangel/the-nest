I suppose this article doubles as a confession–I’m a *somewhat avid yaoi reader.* Not exactly very surprising—give a group of peculiar high schoolers too much time and hormones and this is where things tend to lead.

Anyways....

Wolfsong hooks you, beseeches you to give in to its conditions, tangles strings in your heart, and pulls them apart, slowly, one by one.



The plot follows Ox, a young boy in a fractured family, as he grows and navigates love, loss, and werewolves. As one of TJ Klune’s earlier works, I could see many traces of prominent themes and plot elements that he later would elaborate in other works like found family, trauma. While the work does not share much worldbuilding with The House in the Cerulean Sea (the other work I read), the perspective is distinctively more realistic and grounded. Into Midwestern suburbs, specifically.



The story hurts all the more because of how blithe and naive it starts. Ox starts off living a broken, but bearable life in a small town, where his prospects were controlled, inherited, and frankly disappointing. But, right off the bat, a young boy from his neighbor’s family, the Bennetts, veers his destiny forcibly towards candy canes and pinecones and epic and awesome.



And that encapsulates the first quarter of the book. Fast friends, supportive community-family, and hints at youthful love. It’s so easy to fall into—and makes you thirst for a happy ending. For the natural thing to happen. An assertion that the world can be steered, even in one case, towards a happy direction. Without spoiling anything, I’ll just say that Klune makes you jump hurdles to get there.



Now onto my thoughts. One thing that the book significantly differs from The House in the Cerulean Sea is its perspective in first person. It grounds the storytelling in his inner perception, and things like self-doubt are repeatedly emphasized in prose. It can feel repetitive, but is mostly something you notice oh, this is happening for a reason. It’s one way to build a theme, maybe not the most elegant, but it does provide a building of depth as the same phrases and thought patterns are contrasted to physical growth and plot progression. 



Along with the recurring emphasis, another narrative technique that anchors the emotional background is the outright narration of inner thoughts as concrete nouns. When I first read their resilience and relief being described as green, and their camaraderie being described using a long string of matepackfamilysonbond sort of language, I was like Oh is this a web novel sort of thing? My skepticism, you see, stems from how strange and intriguing these descriptors are when there are perfectly great adjectives to use. But, as I progressed through the book, I found that the entrancing ways that things were described reduced emotions to their core, and that was not only part of the backstory with the whole werewolves have more concentrated emotions setup, but also in some ways reflected Ox’s own characterization as somewhat dense, but genuine and intense.



A quick aside about the smut. It is not overwhelming, and while the book is not avoidant of suggestive scenes or sexual innuendo, the plot does much more than serve the smut. It is not a cheaply written romance for the thrill of the reader; in fact, it tends towards the opposite. In particular, the halfway point was a drag for me because there were solid chapters with pure character development and no intimacy at all. Love is a part of the story, but it is not used lightly. 



On the topic of love, the book explores a certain question many romances avoid entirely, that being coming of age and dynamic shifts in early adulthood. Love stops being a whim or what you feel, but a serious responsibility and commitment. Specifically (with minimal spoiler), the romance develops after a three year absence in which both characters undergo significant physical and mental changes. And they do not leave on great terms, either. A substantial portion of the latter half of the book centers this misalignment in this sort of naive expectation vs. adult reality. Readers could feel cheated on as what started as an innocent teenage romance dove headfirst into something more musky, mixed, heavy, but all the more intense. This distortion of desire and love as part of their growth is more ballast for the emotional weight. One can certainly read it as a tragic loss of innocence and chemistry, but it brushes up on a bigger question; what is the cost of growth, if healing means we have to cut up branches where others used to flower?



I can clearly see why regular, sappy romances would’ve avoided that tension, but Wolfsong clearly does not fit into that category. 



To add to this point, characters do things that are insensible or questionable morally. The book is a series of hard decisions and trolley problems in love, family, and self-worth. Albeit it's a somewhat tried-and-true cliche way to foster suspension, Klune does make us go through them for a reason rather than just trolling or writing trauma-porn. It hurts because you can see each decision leave indelible marks in their interactions, and there’s really no going around that. They never go back to innocent boys that smell like pinecones and taste like awesome, Klune makes that clear. So yes, angsty slow-burn novel as per AO3 standards, or a speculative work of complicated relationships beleaguered by trauma in literary terms.



If I had to critique something, it’d be the characterization of the antagonist. All their problems essentially rest on this rogue Jeffery Epstein type of character, and his whims and plans twist and break everything else. If anything, we do not learn much about his motivations except he’s been treated badly so he wants to get vengeance or something to that line. It makes sense from Ox’s perspective, but given the enormous weight given to the other side of the seesaw—that being their intensely complicated and deep bonds—it feels flat. It is very much a Hero’s Journey sort of story arc in plot, which is fine. But I do feel that The House in the Cerulean Sea’s structure of a local victory to a systemic problem is more insightful in some ways than a classic “rip the head off of the one guy that’s the entire problem” But, to merit the book, it reaches a rather complete resolution and does not depend on the sequels. It would’ve devastated me otherwise. 



In sum, the work is a romance that still feels innocent and burning with desire. But it is also an adventure novel that makes you root for the protagonists and tense at the action. But, at heart, it is a drama that doubles as almost an experiential inquiry asking “how deep does the human heart go?” The book expands to weigh on a multitude of human emotions. It manifests in the insensible simplicity of evil. In the complexity of love. In the estrangement and reconciliation of family. In the love for life, and the love for others in self-sacrifice.



The book says many things about love, but at its very core, love is what heals and makes you whole. Not to make you feel on top of the world, but to feel enough. Even if it does not restore innocence. Even if it cannot fully heal damage, only accommodate it.



And maybe a little bit of *awesome and candy cane and pinecones.*
