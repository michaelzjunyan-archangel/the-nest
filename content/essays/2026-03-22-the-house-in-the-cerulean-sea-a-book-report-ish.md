*The House in the Cerulean Sea*

*Image Credit @Allesia Trunfio on X*

If you are familiar with the internet phrase “This found me at a very \[insert thing] phase of my life,” then suffice it to say, this book found me at a very confused phase in my life. I had just started my second year in high school abroad, been in and out of different friend groups, and did not know what I was going to do with my time. I did not think (and I would not tell my counselor) that reading a comfortasy queer romance would help my situation, and yet it did.

The author of the work, TJ Klune,  is a self-taught American who has been prolific in the genre of LGBTQ+ Romance. In a word, the narrative of his award-winning work follows Linus Baker, an ordinary office worker who finds belonging in a magical orphanage that he is tasked with supervising.

The thing about this book for me is that I don’t consider it romance proper. The characterization of the actual romance arc was so overwhelmingly dwarfed by the themes of belonging, resilience, and found family that the romance barely seems to qualify even as the cherry on top. For me, I felt like the slow-burn and intimacy scenes even felt a bit awkward, like trying to squeeze into an already-bustling dance floor. And it is in those themes, not two men getting together (though certainly there is intrinsic value in the latter as well) that I found myself hung up over the book. 

There are a few merits that I must give to the craftsmanship of the narrative. First of all, the book is rather PG compared to some of his other works (trust me, I’ll get to it in some other review) which I actually consider a plus in this case, because, again, the romance doesn’t take away from the actual narrative. The progression of plot elements and interpersonal relationships feel organic, and not fabricated for shock value. Further, the incorporation of alternative and marginalized characters as main protagonists as something seldom done in mainstream media. Linus being a queer, out-of-shape middle-aged man rather than some impossibly fit, perfect young teenager carries a message in of itself. If anything, beauty deserves to be represented, but it already is and will always be by design. It is of substantial value for authors and directors to instead point the spotlight at the ordinary, the imperfect, and the so-called “unwanted” characters not only because it is reality, but also because one good inspiring story with an ordinary main character is so much more powerful in altering lives than a great inspiring story that feature protagonists with superhuman traits and power overcoming impossible hurdles.

Basically, the characters in this story start off tangled. The main character works an oppressive job and he’s not doing well in any metric of life. His cat doesn’t even love him, he thinks. He meets the children and their guardian at the orphanage, and all of them are also tangled in their own way. Some of them suffered through trauma, others prejudice, and some both. The progression of the plot is really the gradual untangling of all these negative influences, and the rewiring of them back into something stronger and better–into a family. That’s the thing about found family that I never thought could hit so hard–the most unlikely people find this connection from the most unlikely circumstances at first. The story doesn’t try to say that things are okay–because they’re not–but it offers the alternative, the option to have people who will be there for you and help you weather through this difficult world. Even if they’re also…somehow Satan incarnate? (this is canon btw)

The character I particularly enjoy is Sal, a meek boy who also happens to be a shapeshifter–bookworm. He starts off nonverbal and avoidant, but brings himself to be vocal and expressive of his ideas through the help of Linus. I suppose I find a sort of mirror of myself in him, and particularly the tenderness and observant air that he carries. I do believe there are people who are truly mild and tender in that way, but for me it is a somewhat intentional task. I have been so irreversibly hard-wired into the same vernacular, the same knee-jerk remarks that I almost never say anything I would like. But that’s beyond the point. 

Another prominent theme is prejudice. Each protagonist in the book is some sort of magical creature except Linus, who helps perpetrate the heavy segregation between magical beings and muggles (Harry Potter reference because apparently not everyone read it???) that is in place by working for the government. And, as they become evermore accepting and steadfast in their own identities, they march out against this prejudice by exercising their rights. Namely, by buying ice cream from a merchant who refuses them service. They meet people who are so deep in their bigotry they never turn around, but they also meet people who support their cause. 

And that’s the thing about Comfort-Fantasy. No ancient kingdoms fall. No systems break. Conflict is mitigated or resolved at a local level There’s magic, but it is not used in particularly epic ways. The resolution is a localized victory, where their newfound family gets to stay together. Yet it is all the more resounding because it represents a real struggle that is yet one step removed. We don’t feel discomforted or bored by a plot that is hyperrealistic, yet we are connected more than detached to the characters and their world. 

Now onto my own reflection. And the elephant in the room for me may be that this book is perhaps the most significant influence for my own first novel, *Cornflower.* It is hard to say if I would’ve drawn on the same themes–systemic oppression, perfunctory rules, quiet resilience–without reading it. Yet the book brought together many things from my own life and repeated them back to me. I spend most of my time in a place shaped by rules, by prejudice, and by a particular strain of religious conservatism that often felt impossible to escape. It was a lonely environment at times. The book showed me the other side of that narrative. That, perhaps, there was a way out. That the rules need not apply all the time. That there were people who’d still care even when I did not follow every norm placed before me.

And, insofar as the prose went, I think there is a level of mastery in the soothing, almost therapeutic nature of reading it and letting the emotions wash over oneself. In a sense, it showed me those bright spots of the human connection and the human heart by bringing difficulty forward, but nowhere as dark as it could’ve gone. It is important to note that the story was inspired by the Sixties Scoop–a real historical policy in Canada where Indigenous children were removed from their families and placed with white families in what is widely considered a form of cultural genocide–to which end it has received substantial criticism for the way it presented the issue and the lack of indigenous voices in the narrative. Some critics have argued that the novel simplifies or indirectly appropriates this history. Histories like the Sixties Scoop carry generational consequences, and when fiction echoes those events, questions inevitably arise about representation, voice, and responsibility. At the same time, I find that the novel operates less as a direct allegory and more as a moral fable about prejudice and belonging. Its aim seems not to retell the history itself but to gesture toward the broader dynamics of exclusion and institutional control. Whether that approach succeeds is open to debate, but the discussion itself reflects a larger tension within literature: how stories can draw inspiration from real injustices without claiming to speak for the communities who endured them. It is worthwhile to note though that my own experience with the book remained largely separate from that context. The story functions primarily as a work of comfort fantasy rather than historical allegory.

In the end, it didn’t take a lot to read this book, and it made my day at least a little bit brighter.
