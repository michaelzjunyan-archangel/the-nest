WIth an unflattering 2.68/5 on Goodreads, this book might be the first (and perhaps last) experience I had with splatterpunk and extreme horror as a genre. While I have not considered myself an omnivorous reader for some time, I felt the need for a radical palette cleanser, so to speak in spite of the irony of the statement, from all my heartwarming fantasy and romance.



Matthew Stokoe, having published the work in 1998 following its conception in 1992, is a British writer born 1963 who is surprisingly still active on the internet, personally being interviewed and handling translations. 



Without going in detail on the excessive gore and revulsing scenes, Cows is a book about a young man, Steven, abused by his mother since youth, who gains self-awakening and self-proclaimed liberation through learning to kill at a slaughterhouse. It manages to breach almost every taboo in a meager 50,000 words, which is honestly quite impressive considering the relative coherence of the character development. 



Most of the book’s prose is spent on highly unnecessary graphic descriptions of killing, torture, and sexual violence. The beleaguering of the actual plot with these scenes have received a lot of backfire, though I suppose it is a common element and selling point of this genre. 



However, I would like to discuss in some depth the plot and theme (if one may fathom there is any) of the work. Steven abuses the TV as a window to his aspirations, which he tries and fails to reach. The non-violent parts of the book are essentially a chronicle of his repeated attempts at normalization into his view of a more ideal life, which are subsequently caught short by marginalizing acts either on his behalf or by others. Namely, he maintains an abusive relationship with Lucy, his mentally ill girlfriend—weaponizing his own experience to manipulate her into a pawn for his visionary goals at a “normal, happy life.” Just as he found closure in the hurdles of a relationship, she commits suicide out of her own obsession. 



This theme that killing inevitably leads to one degrading others to tools that can be violated is a powerful message the book tries to convey, though I hardly think it does it very well. Stokoe clearly gets carried away by the mere shock value of writing more and more absurd scenes, mitigating any growth or difficulty in Steven’s own narrative. He cheats on his girlfriend with a heifer, commands a herd of sentient cows, and all the while the walls of his apartment collapse without the building falling. There is weight behind his actions and a clear, poignant motive, but the incomprehensible clash with the \[gory random scenes] make the plot progression hardly able to be more than an inadequate medium for more gore to happen. 



That is especially disappointing, considering the prose and metaphor carry a certain lyricism that blends cynical views, postmodern desperation, and an absurdist-surrealist bizarreness. 



“\[Lucy] was the hook on which to hang his plagiarized blueprint for living.”



“Having been inside its master’s body, \[The regurgitated food] was sacrament to the animal, and their consumption scorched its brain with dreams of becoming man”



Steven’s world is one where he constantly seeks meaning and some sort of fulfillment, to which none is given by the cruel dystopia he inhabits. He kills to fill that hole, except it keeps getting bigger. But we are given no resolution to that either. 



This shock and splatter largely undermine the literary value, but then again without things like “No. 1 most disturbing book” that brings it to the forefront of discussion it hardly may garner this level of cultural visibility. It may not have been written to grab attention–but it is certainly marketed as so. I don’t think it is problematic—the gore does more to drive people away than pull others toward violence or get under people's skin, really. I didn’t find it disturbing at all, just twisted with upended morals and absurd, calculated grotesque. If one embarks to look for the themes and subtleties, then be assured they’ll have a while of excavation to do.
